The structured 5-step approach top FRC teams use. Calculate load → derive current → verify budget. Built-in calculators for the common cases; links to ReCalc/JVN for advanced ones.

1Inventory by function, not by motor

Don't list "8 Krakens." List functions: drivetrain, shooter, intake, hopper/uptake, turret, climber, fixed loads. Each function aggregates its motors and becomes one budget row. This is what the Budget tab is organized around.

2Derive current from the load (don't guess)

For each function, calculate the physical load (torque, force, or energy), then translate through gear ratio + motor curve to get current. Never pick current limits by "feels right" — they should be 1.5× calculated peak.

Motor curves loaded: Kraken X60/X44, Minion, Falcon 500, NEO, NEO Vortex, NEO 550. Calculators below cover the 6 most common mechanism types.

⚡ Stator current vs Supply current — which do you use?
Stator = through the motor windings, produces torque (τ = K_t · I). Used for motor protection and stator current limits.
Supply = what the battery delivers (I_supply ≈ I_stator · duty, where duty = (I·R + K_t·ω) / 12 V). Used for budget, sag math, PDH totals, and the 180 A ceiling.
Rule of thumb: at high torque + low RPM (stalled motor), supply is much smaller than stator. At high RPM near no-load, supply ≈ stator. Each calculator below shows both.
For Step 3's simultaneity matrix: use SUPPLY values.

Adambots 2026 shooter has two wheels on a shared belt/shaft: flywheel (3.0 lb, 6.0 in²·lb) + shooter wheel (1.6 lb, 3.2 in²·lb). The motor sees the sum. Set the second MOI to 0 for single-wheel setups. Click 📐 to convert from kg·m² if pulling from a physics sim.

Pick Target RPM from your interpolation table, not motor free speed. Adambots 2026 tops out around 62 RPS (3720 RPM) for the 5 m / lob shot (kLobShotRPS = 62, kMaxRPS ≈ 62 + kShotBoostRPS = 3). Default 4000 RPM bakes in ~7 % headroom. Peak current scales linearly with ω_target — sizing at 5000 RPM when you never run there inflates the current limit.

Why my "peak current" is lower than ReCalc's "60 A limit": this tool uses the linearized torque-current curve (τ = K_t · i). ReCalc models back-EMF, which dramatically reduces available torque near no-load speed — so its current draw at high RPM is higher than the pure-inertia calc predicts. For spin-up time or projectile-impact sizing, use reca.lc/flywheel. Use this tool for the simultaneity matrix and budget sanity.

Adambots 2026 intake arm: Minion + 20:1 planetary + 2:1 belt = 40:1 total (IntakeConstants.kArmTotalGearRatio). Arm travels 90° between raised (193°) and lowered (100°). At motor cruise 6 RPS through 40:1, arm speed is 0.15 RPS ≈ 54°/s, so a full deploy takes ~1.5 s — not 0.5 s.

Current-limit sizing rule for arms (vs. flywheels): the "abnormal peak" isn't back-EMF, it's mechanical catch (bumper hit, roller jam kickback, FUEL jammed between intake and chassis). Add 30–50 % headroom above calc peak — not 2× like the flywheel — because gravity is the dominant steady torque and we don't want the limit fighting gravity during a hold.

Two ceilings on accel: traction (slip current ~100 A/motor — above this, wheels spin in place) and stator limit (electrical cap we choose). Whichever is lower binds.

Adambots 2026 runs stator = 40 A/motor (physicalproperties.json: currentLimit.drive: 40) — well below slip. That means we're stator-limited, not traction-limited: accel caps around 4 m/s² because the controller refuses to push more current, not because the wheels slip. Trade-off is deliberate — at 4 motors × 40 A = 160 A drive-only, we stay under the 180 A sustained ceiling when shooter + intake fire simultaneously. Raising stator to ~60–70 A would unlock ~6 m/s² accel at the cost of more battery sag.

Set stator limit ≈ slip to unlock full traction; keep supply limit lower (~40A) to cap battery draw.

Adambots 2026 uses a modified cone on a 3:1 belt drive (the hopperMotor in code, even though mechanically it's the spindexer). The calc below models rotational body as a solid disk (I = ½·m·r²); this overestimates MOI vs. a cone (I = 3/10·m·r²) and a slope's friction, so the numbers are a conservative upper bound.

Pieces are point masses at r_piece. Friction μ 0.3–0.5 typical for rubber-on-plastic. FUEL mass 0.5 lb. For a cone mechanism, put the cone's total mass and base radius in the two body fields. Piece radius ≤ base radius unless pieces ride in a retaining ring outboard of the cone.

Stall current matters here — spindexers work against jams. Pick stator limit with headroom for a brief stall.

4Apply practical ceilings

If your worst-case row exceeds 180 A sustained, options in order of least invasiveness: sequence operations (auto-stop intake when shooting), lower supply limits on secondary functions, lower setpoints (slower shooter spool-up), redesign gearing / motor selection.

5Verify against battery with sag math

For each simultaneity row, predict voltage: V_loaded = V_rest − I · R_battery. Flag any scenario where V < 7.5 V as high-risk.

The Budget tab does this automatically once you transfer your scenario numbers. Use the Scenarios buttons to activate groups and watch the voltage metric.

Retire any battery whose IR makes any realistic scenario drop below 7.5 V — not the worst case, but any scenario you'll actually hit in a match.
